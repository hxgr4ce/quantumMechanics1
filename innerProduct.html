Properties of the Inner Product

Geometric meaning of the Dot Product

The inner product extends the concept of dot products/scalar products of Cartesian coordinate vectors to possibly infinite-dimensional and complex spaces. Say that we have vectors \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle=\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}\) in \((\mathbb{R}^2, \mathbb{R})\), which look like: \(\langle\vec{u}\mid\vec{v}\rangle = 2\), which is the length of the component of \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle\) parallel to \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\).
Say instead that \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle=\begin{pmatrix}2\\3\end{pmatrix}\), in which case our two vectors look like this: \(\langle\vec{u}\mid\vec{v}\rangle = 7\), which is clearly not the length of the component of \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle\) parallel to \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\). It's actually that length multiplied by the length of \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\). The length of \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle\) along \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\) is then \(\frac{7}{\sqrt13}\).

In our previous example, the length of \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\) was 1, so we missed this particular nuance...

The Action of the Inner Product

For two vectors \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\) and \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle\) of dimension \(n\), the inner product \(\langle\vec{u}\mid\vec{v}\rangle\) is \(\Sigma_{i=1}^n u_i^*v_i\) for each element \(u_i\) and \(v_i\) in \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\) and \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle\).

The Inner Product of Continuous Functions

If we have a continuous function, that is a function that is continuous for all possible inputs, then it must have infinitely many possible output values along the interval on which it is defined. We can then represent these continuous functions as infinite-dimensional vector.

Given two continous functions, \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) defined along the inerval \([a,b]\), the inner product of their vector representations is: \[ \begin{align} \langle\vec{f}\mid\vec{g}\rangle &= \int_a^b f^*(x)g(x)dx \end{align} \] Which is essentially the same as the dot product, just defined over a continuous set of possible \(x\).

Orthonormal vectors

As defined earlier, two vectors in a vector space \(V\) are orthogonal if they have an inner product of 0. A set of vectors \(S=\{\mid\vec{v_i}\rangle\}\) is an orthogonal set of all vectors in \(S\) are orthogonal to all other vectors in \(S\).

If each vectors in \(S\) is also normalized (has a length of 1), then \(S\) is an orthonormal set.

The most common orthonormalization process (a process which generates a set of orthonormal vectors) is Gram-Schmidt Orthonormalization, which follows the following process:
  1. Pick an arbitrary vector \(\mid\vec{v_1}\rangle\) and normalize it to create: \(\mid\vec{e_1}\rangle=\frac{\mid\vec{v_1}\rangle}{\sqrt{\langle\vec{v_1}\mid\vec{v_1}\rangle}}\)
  2. Pick a vector \(\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle\) and select its component orthogonal to \(\mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\) (and \(\mid\vec{v_1}\rangle\), since they are parallel): \(\mid\vec{v_{2\perp}}\rangle = \mid\vec{v_2}\rangle - \mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\langle\vec{e_1}\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle\).

    Note that since \(\mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\) is normalized, we don't have to account for its length in the term \(\mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\langle\vec{e_1}\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle\).
  3. Normalize \(\mid\vec{v_{2\perp}}\rangle\) to get \(\mid\vec{e_2}\rangle=\frac{\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle - \mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\langle\vec{e_1}\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle}{\sqrt{\langle\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle - \mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\langle\vec{e_1}\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle\mid\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle - \mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\langle\vec{e_1}\mid\vec{v_2}\rangle\rangle}}\)
  4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 until you have all orthonormal vectors.

    Note that in step 2, you must select the component of your vector that is orthogonal to all other vectors you have created so far: \(\mid\vec{v_{i\perp}}\rangle = \mid\vec{v_i}\rangle - \mid\vec{e_1}\rangle\langle\vec{e_1}\mid\vec{v_i}\rangle- \mid\vec{e_2}\rangle\langle\vec{e_2}\mid\vec{v_i}\rangle-...- \mid\overrightarrow{e_{i-1}}\rangle\langle\overrightarrow{e_{i-1}}\mid\vec{v_i}\rangle\)

Schwarz Inequality

The Schwarz inequality says that for a vector space \(V\): \[ \forall \mid\vec{u}\rangle, \mid\vec{v}\rangle \in V, \mid\langle\vec{u}\mid\vec{v}\rangle\mid \leq ||\mid\vec{u}\rangle||\cdot||\mid\vec{v}\rangle|| \] or equivalently, \[ \forall \mid\vec{u}\rangle, \mid\vec{v}\rangle \in V, \mid\langle\vec{u}\mid\vec{v}\rangle\mid^2 \leq \langle\vec{u}\mid\vec{u}\rangle\cdot\langle\vec{v}\mid\vec{v}\rangle \] Essentially this states that the magnitude of the projection of \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle\) onto some \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\) (if \(\mid\vec{u}\rangle\) has unit length) must be less than the length of \(\langle\vec{v}\mid\vec{v}\rangle\).

Triangle Inequality

The triangle inequality says that for a vector space \(V\): \[ \forall \mid\vec{u}\rangle, \mid\vec{v}\rangle \in V, || \mid\overrightarrow{u+v}\rangle ||^2 \leq ||\mid\vec{u}\rangle||+||\mid\vec{v}\rangle|| \] Which mirrors the normal geometric triangle inequality.
