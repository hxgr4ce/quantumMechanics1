Derivation of Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

Say that we have observables \(A\) and \(B\). They may or may not commute. We start by defining two functions/vectors: \(\mid\phi_A\rangle=(A-\langle A\rangle)\mid\psi\rangle\) and \(\mid\phi_B\rangle=(B-\langle B\rangle)\mid\psi\rangle\) which take existing state \(\mid\psi\rangle\) as input.

According to the Schwarz inequality, we know that \[|\langle \phi_A\mid\phi_B\rangle| \leq||\mid\phi_A\rangle||\cdot||\mid\phi_B\rangle||\] Let's work on the right hand side of this inequality first: \[\begin{align} ||\mid\phi_A\rangle||^2 &= \langle\phi_A\mid\phi_A\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi\mid(A-\langle A\rangle)^2\mid\psi\rangle \\ &= \sigma_A^2 \end{align}\] (see the definition of standard deviation) and similarly, \[\begin{align} ||\mid\phi_B\rangle||^2 &= \sigma_B^2 \end{align}\] Now, on the left hand side: \[\begin{align} \langle\phi_A\mid\phi_B\rangle = \langle\psi\mid(A-\langle A\rangle)(B-\langle B\rangle)\mid\psi\rangle \end{align}\] for convenience let us refer to \(A-\langle A\rangle\) as \(\Delta A\) and \(B-\langle B\rangle\) as \(\Delta B\) so that we have: \[\begin{align} \langle\phi_A\mid\phi_B\rangle = \langle\psi\mid\Delta A\Delta B\mid\psi\rangle \tag{1} \end{align}\] \(\Delta A\Delta B\) can be written as: \[\begin{align} \Delta A\Delta B &= \frac{1}{2}(\Delta A\Delta B+\Delta B\Delta A) + \frac{1}{2}\frac{i}{i}(\Delta A\Delta B-\Delta B\Delta A) \tag{2}\\ \end{align}\] each of these terms are self-adjoint: \[\begin{align} (\Delta A\Delta B+\Delta B\Delta A)^\dagger &= \Delta B^\dagger \Delta A^\dagger + \Delta A^\dagger\Delta B^\dagger \\ &= \Delta B\Delta A + \Delta A\Delta B \\ (\Delta A\Delta B-\Delta B\Delta A)^\dagger &= \Delta B^\dagger \Delta A^\dagger - \Delta A^\dagger\Delta B^\dagger \\ &= \Delta B\Delta A - \Delta A\Delta B \end{align}\] this means that the value of \(\langle\phi_A\mid\phi_B\rangle\) must be real, by the spectral theorem. If we subsititute (2) into (1), we get: \[\begin{align} \langle\phi_A\mid\phi_B\rangle &= \langle\psi\mid\Delta A\Delta B\mid\psi\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi\mid\frac{1}{2}(\Delta A\Delta B+\Delta B\Delta A) + \frac{1}{2}\frac{i}{i}(\Delta A\Delta B-\Delta B\Delta A)\mid\psi\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi\mid\frac{1}{2}(\Delta A\Delta B+\Delta B\Delta A)\mid\psi\rangle + \frac{i}{2}\langle\psi\mid(\frac{\Delta A\Delta B-\Delta B\Delta A}{i})\mid\psi\rangle \\ &= \frac{1}{2}\langle\psi\mid(\{\Delta A,\Delta B\})\mid\psi\rangle + \frac{i}{2}\langle\psi\mid(\frac{[\Delta A,\Delta B]}{i})\mid\psi\rangle \\ |\langle\phi_A\mid\phi_B\rangle|^2 &= |\frac{1}{2}\langle\psi\mid(\{\Delta A,\Delta B\})\mid\psi\rangle + \frac{i}{2}\langle\psi\mid(\frac{[\Delta A,\Delta B]}{i})\mid\psi\rangle|^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{4}|\langle\psi\mid\{\Delta A,\Delta B\}\mid\psi\rangle|^2 +\frac{1}{4}|\langle\psi\mid[\Delta A,\Delta B]\mid\psi\rangle|^2 \\ &\geq \frac{1}{4}|\langle\psi\mid[\Delta A,\Delta B]\mid\psi\rangle|^2 \end{align}\] (Note that because we're taking the complex conjugate squared, the \(i\) terms disappear!) We can plug in everything we've derived back into the Schwarz inequality, to get: \[\begin{align} \frac{1}{4}|\langle[\Delta A,\Delta B]\rangle|^2 &\leq \sigma_A^2\sigma_B^2 \\ \frac{1}{2}|\langle[\Delta A,\Delta B]\rangle| &\leq \sigma_A\sigma_B \\ \end{align}\] The last thing we need to determine is the value of \([\Delta A,\Delta B]\): \[\begin{align} [\Delta A,\Delta B] &= [A-\langle A\rangle, B-\langle B\rangle] \\ &= [A,B] -[\langle A\rangle,B] - [A,\langle B\rangle] + [\langle A\rangle,\langle B\rangle] \end{align}\] An expectation value is just a scalar. For an arbitrary scalar \(c\), say we have observable \(O\). \([c, O]=cO-Oc=0\), and \([0, c]=Oc-cO=0\), so the commutator between an operator and a scalar is 0. So, \[[\Delta A,\Delta B]=[A,B]\] and we finally arrive at Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle: \[\begin{align} \frac{1}{2}|\langle[A, B]\rangle| &\leq \sigma_A\sigma_B \\ \end{align}\]
