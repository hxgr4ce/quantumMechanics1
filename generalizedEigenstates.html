Generalized Eigenstates

When we transitioned to quantum mechanics, we saw that we work with generalized eigenstates; mathematical devices that are not normalizable, nor in the Hilbert space, and do not represent a physical quantum state.

Instead, they let us map states in a Hilbert space, \(|\psi\rangle\) to functions that are square integrable, e.g. \(\langle p|\psi\rangle, \langle x|p\rangle \in L^2(\mathbb{R})\) for generalized eigenstates \(|x\rangle\) and \(|p\rangle\) such that \(\hat x|x\rangle = x|x\rangle\) and \(\hat p|p\rangle = p|p\rangle\). (See note here about how the Hilbert space is isomorphic to the space \(L^2(\mathbb{R})\).)

Given that we know \(|\psi\rangle \in H\), and that \(\int dx |x\rangle\langle x|=\hat 1\), \[\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx |x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle = \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx |x\rangle \psi(x) \in H\] (even if \(\langle x|\psi\rangle = \psi(x)\) is not!)

Taking a look into the relationship between \(\langle p|\psi\rangle = \phi_\psi(p) = \phi(p)\) and \(\langle x|\psi\rangle = \psi_\psi(x) = \psi(x)\): \[\begin{align} \phi_\psi(p) = \phi(p) = \langle p|\psi\rangle &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx \langle p|x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{-ipx/\hbar}\psi(x) \tag{1} \\ \psi_\psi(x) = \psi(x) = \langle x|\psi\rangle &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp \langle x|p\rangle\langle p|\psi\rangle \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(x) \tag{2} \end{align}\] where (1) is the Fourier transform of \(\psi(x)\) to momentum space and (2) is the inverse Fourier Transform back to position space.

We can also substitute (2) into (1) to get: \[ \psi(x) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{ipx/\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x') \] We can prove this relationship is true without using any of the above relationships. Starting with the right hand side: \[\int_{-\infty}^\infty dp \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{ipx/\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x')\tag{3}\]

The Quick Way

We start by swapping the integrals, since this yields the convenient result: \[ \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar} \] but \(e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}\) is not integrable! So we introduce some regularization factor that makes the integrand 0 at \(-\infty\) and \(\infty\): \(e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar}\), and then later we simply take the limit as \(\lambda \to 0\) so that our introduced term goes away again: Our integrand is now: \[e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar}\] and when we take the integral we get: \[\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar} &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar-\lambda|p|/\hbar} \\ &= \int_{0}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar-\lambda p/\hbar} + \int_{-\infty}^0 dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar+\lambda p/\hbar} \end{align}\] (note that when we split the integral into positive and negative portions, we flip the sign of the exponential term \(-\lambda p/\hbar \to \lambda p/\hbar\) in the negative portion because the bounds of integration would make \(p\) negative, but in that specific term we want it to be positive). \[\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar} &= \int_{0}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar-\lambda p/\hbar} + \int_{-\infty}^0 dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar+\lambda p/\hbar} \\ &= \frac{1}{\frac{i(x-x')}{\hbar}-\frac{\lambda}{\hbar}}(e^\infty-e^0) + \frac{1}{\frac{i(x-x')}{\hbar}+\frac{\lambda}{\hbar}}(e^0-e^{-\infty}) \\ &= \frac{\hbar}{i(x-x')-\lambda}(-1) + \frac{\hbar}{i(x-x')+\lambda}(1) \\ &= \frac{2\hbar\lambda}{(x-x')^2+\lambda^2} \tag{4} \end{align}\] Plugging in our regularization factor and then (4): \[\begin{align} \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar} &\to \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar} \tag{5} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \frac{2\hbar\lambda}{(x-x')^2+\lambda^2} \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \frac{\lambda}{(x-x')^2+\lambda^2} \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx'}{\lambda} \psi(x') \frac{\lambda^2}{(x-x')^2+\lambda^2} \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx'}{\lambda} \psi(x') \frac{1}{\frac{(x-x')}{\lambda}^2+1} \\ \end{align}\] say that \(u = \frac{x-x'}{\lambda}\), so \(du = \frac{dx'}{\hbar}\) and \(x' = x + u\lambda\): \[\begin{align} \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar} &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx'}{\lambda} \psi(x') \frac{1}{\frac{(x-x')}{\lambda}^2+1} \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty du \psi(x+u\lambda) \frac{1}{u^2+1} \\ \end{align}\] And we want to remove the regularization constant that we added initially: \[\begin{align} \lim_{\lambda\to 0} \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty du \psi(x+u\lambda) \frac{1}{u^2+1} &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty du \psi(x) \frac{1}{u^2+1} \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi}\psi(x)\arctan(u)\big|_{-\infty}^{\infty} \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi}\psi(x)(\frac{\pi}{2}-(-\frac{\pi}{2})) \\ &= \psi(x) \end{align}\] so, we prove what we set out to prove. Also, if we take a look at the entire equation after swapping the order of integration: \[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar} \] we see that the expression \(\frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}\) must be \(\delta(x-x')\).

The long way (that is more justified)

For some double integral: \[\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx \int_{-\infty}^\infty dy f(x,y)\] we can ONLY swap the order of integration if \[\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx \int_{-\infty}^\infty dy |f(x,y)| \lt\infty\] in other words, if the integrand is absolutely integrable. In our case, the integrand is \(e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}\psi(x')\), and \(|e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar}\psi(x')| = |\psi(x')|\). Because \(\psi(x')\) is not in terms of \(p\), \[\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx \int_{-\infty}^\infty dy |\psi(x')| = \infty\] so we cannot, in fact, just swap the order of integration just like that. What we can do is recast the integral so that the integrand eventually behaves like we want it to: For some \(f(x', p)\), if we define \(f_\lambda(x',p) \equiv e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar}f(x',p)\), then \[f(x',p) = \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar} f(x', p)\] pointwise. We apply this to the right hand side of (3): \[\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{ipx/\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x') &= \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ipx/\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x') \end{align}\] but we still can't swap the order of integration because we have a limit between the two integrals! We now have to pull out the limit, which we can do by the Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT).

The Dominated Convergence Theorem says that you can swap the integral and a limit: \[\int\lim_{n\to\infty}F_n(x) dx = \lim_{n\to\infty}\int F_n(x) dx\] only if the the following conditions are met:
  • Say we have a family of functions \(F_n(x):\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{C}\) parameterized by \(n\), which is continuous.
  • \(\exists F | \lim_{n\to\infty}F_n = F\) pointwise.
  • \(\exists G\) which is positive and integrable (so absolutely integrable) such that \(|F_n(x)| \leq G(x) \forall x\in\mathbb{R}\), since \(\mathbb{R}\) is the domain of \(F_n(x)\).

For \(F_\lambda (p) = e^{-\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ipx/\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx'e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x')\), we know that \(|F_lambda(p)|\leq|\phi(p)|c\), where \(c\) is some constant, so we can indeed swap the limit and integral to get: \[\begin{align} &\frac{1}{2\pi\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ipx/\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x') \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar} \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ipx/\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x') \\ \end{align}\] and finally we can swap the order of integration: \[\begin{align} &\frac{1}{2\pi\hbar} \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ipx/\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' e^{-ipx'/\hbar}\psi(x') \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar} \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ipx/\hbar}e^{-ipx'/\hbar} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar} \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar} \end{align}\] We can now proceed to solve this just like we did from (5) onwards, until we get to the expression: \[\lim_{\lambda\to 0} \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty du \psi(x+u\lambda) \frac{1}{u^2+1} \] where we can justify swapping the limit and integral again by once again invoking DCT, since \(\frac{\psi(x+\lambda u)}{u^2+1} \leq \frac{max|\psi(x)|}{u^2+1}\). From this, we can do: \[\begin{align} \lim_{\lambda\to 0} \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty du \psi(x+u\lambda) \frac{1}{u^2+1} &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty \lim_{\lambda\to 0} \psi(x+u\lambda) \frac{du}{u^2+1} \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty \psi(x) \frac{du}{u^2+1} \\ &= \psi(x) \end{align}\] and we again also see that if \[\psi(x) = \lim_{\lambda\to 0^+} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar} \] then \(\frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar} = \delta(x-x')\).

Functional Analysis?

Conceptually, if you are familiar with functional analysis (I'm not), you may see the connection with some linear functional \(\hat L\), which takes a function \(f\) and evaluates it at some specific \(x\), so that \(\hat Lf = f(x)\). From what I understand, you can use \(F(x) = \int k(x, x')f(x')dx'\) as \(\hat Lf(x)\), where \(k(x,x')\) is \(\delta(x-x')\). \[\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^\infty dp e^{\lambda|p|/\hbar}e^{ip(x-x')/\hbar} &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx' \psi(x') \delta(x-x') \end{align}\] fits this form, which I suppose further reinforces that the above expression is equal to \(\psi(x)\).

Generalized Eigenstates and Probability

We now know a little bit more about the mathematical form of the wave functions that generalized eigenstates can create. Do they have any physical meaning?

Say that we have \(\hat x|x\rangle = x|x\rangle\). For non-generalized eigenstate \(|x\rangle\), we know that \(|x\rangle\langle x|\) is a projector that takes in an arbitrary state vector and returns an eigenvector that represents a state which corresponds to eigenvalue \(x\).

For generalized eigenstates, however, \(|x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle, |\psi\rangle\in H\ = |x\rangle\psi(x)\) which is not an eigenvector that represents some state, and is not in Hilbert space \(H\), because its norm diverges: \[\begin{align} || |x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle ||^2 &= \langle \psi|x\rangle\langle x|x\rangle \langle x|\psi\rangle = |\psi(x)|^2\langle x|x\rangle \\ &= |\psi(x)|^2\delta(0) = \infty \\ || |x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle || &= \infty \end{align}\] Recall that we now have continuous eigenvalues, and that \(\langle x|x'\rangle = \delta(x-x')\), which is not a finite scalar product anymore! So, \(|x\rangle\langle x|\) is not a projector.

For this reason, we can't construct a projector onto a single eigenvalue \(|x\rangle\). We must instead define a projector over an interval of the continuous spectra: \[\begin{align} \hat P_{[a,b]} &= \int_a^b |x\rangle\langle x|dx \\ \hat P_{[a,b]}|\psi\rangle &= \int_a^b |x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle dx = \int_a^b |x\rangle\psi(x) dx \end{align}\] which has a norm that does not diverge: \[\begin{align} || \int_a^b |x\rangle\psi(x) dx ||^2 &= \langle \int_a^b |x\rangle\psi(x) dx | \int_a^b |x'\rangle\psi(x') dx' \rangle \\ &= (\int_a^b |x\rangle\psi(x) dx)^* (\int_a^b |x'\rangle\psi(x') dx' \rangle) \\ &= \int_a^b \langle x | \psi^*(x) dx \int_a^b |x'\rangle\psi(x') dx' \\ &= \int_a^b \int_a^b \langle x | \psi^*(x) dx |x'\rangle\psi(x') dx' \\ &= \int_a^b \int_a^b \psi^*(x) \psi(x') \langle x |x'\rangle dx \text{ } dx' \\ &= \int_a^b \int_a^b \psi^*(x) \psi(x') \delta(x-x') dx \text{ } dx' \\ &= \int_a^b \psi^*(x) \psi(x) dx = \int_a^b|\psi(x)|^2 dx \\ \end{align}\] Since we know that \(\langle x|\psi\rangle \in L^2(\mathbb{R})\), or that it is square integrable, \(\int_a^b|\psi(x)|^2 dx\) does not diverge, so the norm does not diverge, and we are still within the Hilbert space. (Note that the delta function collapses the double integral into one single integral towards the end of the above steps...) This also reinforces the fact that we now use \(\hat 1 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty |x\rangle\langle x| dx\).

Given that \(\psi\rangle\) is normalized, the associated probabilty when taking a measurement that corresponds to some \(\hat P_{[a,b]}\) is: \[\begin{align} p_{[a,b]} = || \hat P_{[a,b]} |\psi\rangle ||^2 &= \langle \hat P_{[a,b]} \psi | \hat P_{[a,b]}\psi \rangle = \langle \psi |\hat P_{[a,b]} |\psi \rangle \\ &= \langle \psi | \int_a^b |x\rangle\langle x | \psi \rangle \\ &= \int_a^b \langle \psi | x \rangle\langle x | \psi \rangle = \int_a^b |\psi(x)|^2 dx \\ \end{align}\] For an interval \([a-\frac{\Delta x}{2}, a+\frac{\Delta x}{2}]\), the probability is: \[p_{[a-\frac{\Delta x}{2}, a+\frac{\Delta x}{2}]} = \int_{a-\frac{\Delta x}{2}}^{a+\frac{\Delta x}{2}} |\psi(x)|^2 dx = |\psi(a)|^2\cdot\Delta x + o(\Delta x^3)\] and as we make \(\Delta x\) smaller and smaller: \[\lim_{\Delta x\to 0} p_{[a-\frac{\Delta x}{2}, a+\frac{\Delta x}{2}]} = \lim_{\Delta x\to 0}|\psi(a)|^2\cdot\Delta x + o(\Delta x^3)\] \[\lim_{\Delta x\to 0} \frac{p_{[a-\frac{\Delta x}{2}, a+\frac{\Delta x}{2}]}}{\Delta x}= \lim_{\Delta x\to 0}|\psi(a)|^2 = |\psi(a)|^2 \tag{6}\] This tells us that any \(\psi(x) = \langle x | \psi\rangle \) tells us a probability! More specifically, (6) tells us that \(|\psi(x)|^2\) is a probability density, just like \(|x\rangle\langle x|\) is a projector density. In fact, \(|\psi(x)|^2 = \big\langle|x\rangle\langle x|\big\rangle_\psi\)

Approximating Eigenstates

If we have some generalied eigenstate that is not in the Hilbert space, we can still approximate it with some family of functions that is in the Hilbert space. Namely, for each eigenvalue of \(\hat A\), one can find as many vectors in the Hilbert space as there are corresponding eigenvectors \(|\lambda\rangle\) such that \(\hat A|\lambda\rangle = \lambda|\lambda\rangle\), with some non-zero length where \[\lim_{n\to\infty}|| \hat A |\psi_{\lambda,n}\rangle - \lambda |\psi_{\lambda,n}\rangle|| = 0\] In this case, \(\lambda\) represents the associated eigenvalue, and \(n\) is the index of the vector within the family of functions in Hilbert space. Formally, \[\forall \lambda \in \sigma_c(\hat A), \exists |\psi_{\lambda,n}\rangle \in H, || |\psi_{\lambda, n}\rangle || \gt c \gt 0\] \[\lim_{n\to\infty}|| \hat A |\psi_{\lambda,n}\rangle - \lambda |\psi_{\lambda,n}\rangle|| = 0\] for some constant c.

As an example, we can use the Gaussian function as a wave function: \[\psi_{\sigma, x_0}(x) = \langle x |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\] where \(|\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle \in H\), and \(\sigma\) is inversely proportional to \(n\), so we want the limit as \(\sigma\) goes to 0, instead of infinity. This \(|\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle\) meets the conditions needed to approximate the eigenstate for some eigenvalue \(x_0\). It is in the Hilbert space, and its norm is non-zero: \[\begin{align} || |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle||^2 &= \langle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} | \psi_{\sigma, x_0} \rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx \langle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} |x\rangle\langle x| \psi_{\sigma, x_0} \rangle \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}(x)|^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx e^{-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{\sigma^2}} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} \sigma\sqrt{\pi} = \frac{1}{2\sigma\sqrt{\pi}} \\ || |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle|| &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{2\sigma\sqrt{\pi}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\sigma\sqrt{\pi}}} \end{align}\] We can also enforce that \[\lim_{\sigma\to 0}|| \hat x |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle - x_0 |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle|| = 0\] meaning that it does indeed approximate the generalized eigenstate: \[\begin{align} || \hat x |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle - x_0 |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle||^2 &= || (\hat x - x_0)|\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle ||^2 \\ &= \langle (\hat x - x_0)\psi_{\sigma, x_0} | (\hat x - x_0)\psi_{\sigma, x_0} \rangle = \langle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} |(\hat x - x_0)^2 | \psi_{\sigma, x_0} \rangle \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\langle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} |(\hat x - x_0)^2 |x\rangle\langle x| \psi_{\sigma, x_0} \rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\langle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} |(x - x_0)^2 |x\rangle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} (x) \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}(x - x_0)^2 \langle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} |x\rangle \psi_{\sigma, x_0} (x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}(x - x_0)^2 \psi_{\sigma, x_0}^*(x) \psi_{\sigma, x_0} (x) \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}(x - x_0)^2 e^{-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{\sigma^2}} dx \\ \text{Say } u = x-x_0, du &= dx \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} u^2 e^{-\frac{u^2}{\sigma^2}} dx \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\frac{1}{2} (\frac{1}{\sigma^2})^{-3/2}\sqrt{\pi} = \frac{1}{4\sqrt{\pi}\sigma^2} \sigma^3 = \frac{\sigma}{4\sqrt{\pi}} \\ || \hat x |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle - x_0 |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle|| &= \sqrt{\frac{\sigma}{4\sqrt{\pi}}} = \frac{\sqrt \sigma}{2\pi^{1/4}} \end{align}\] So, we see that: \[\lim_{\sigma\to 0}|| \hat x |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle - x_0 |\psi_{\sigma, x_0}\rangle|| = \lim_{\sigma\to 0} \frac{\sqrt \sigma}{2\pi^{1/4}} = 0\] As \(\sigma\to 0\), the spread of the Gaussian gets narrower and narrower, while the curve stays centered around \(x_0\). We also see that \[\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \psi_{\sigma, x_0}(x) dx &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \\ &= \frac{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} \\ &= 1 \end{align}\] which tells us that, in fact, this Gaussian is approaching the Dirac delta, which jumps up to infinity at \(x_0\) (\(\psi_{\sigma, x_0}(x_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\), which goes to \(\infty\) as \(\sigma\) gets small) and has an integral of 1.

For any other value of \(x\) that is not extremely close to \(x_0\), the Gaussian function returns 0, so we can say that as \(\sigma \to 0\), \(\langle x|x'\rangle = \delta(x-x')\), which reflects what we disccused earlier on this page. We can also say that the probability of measuring a value that is extreme close (or equal to) \(x_0\) is 1.
