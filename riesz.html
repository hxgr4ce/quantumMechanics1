Riesz Representation Theorem for Finite-Dimensional Spaces

Say that we have linear functional \(f: V \to \mathbb{C}, \mid\vec{v}\rangle \mapsto f\mid\vec{v}\rangle\) for a complex vector space \(V\) with orthonormal basis \(\{\vec{\mid e_i \rangle}\}_{i=1,...,n}\), and that the linear application for dual correspondence is \(C\).

Since \(f\) is linear, when it acts on an arbitrary vector \(\mid\vec{v}\rangle\), it acts individually on the orthonormal basis of \(V\): \[ \begin{align} \vec{v} &= \Sigma_{i=1}^n c_i\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle \tag{1}\\ f\mid\vec{v}\rangle &= f(\Sigma_{i=1}^n c_i\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle) \\ &= \Sigma_{i=1}^n c_if(\vec{\mid e_i\rangle}) \tag{2} \end{align} \] Since \(f\) maps to the the complex field \(\mathbb{C}\), \(f(\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle)\) is some complex value, let's call it \(\alpha_i\) for now. This means that we can write: \[f\mid\vec{v}\rangle = \Sigma_{i=1}^n c_i \alpha_i \tag{3}\]

Let's now consider another vector, \(\mid\vec{w}\rangle\), which is defined by the complex conjugate of \(\{\alpha_i\}_{i=1,...,n}\), \(\{\alpha_i^*\}_{i=1,...,n}\): \[ \begin{align} \mid\vec{w}\rangle &= \Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i^* \mid\vec{e_i}\rangle \\ \langle\vec{w}\mid &= C\mid\vec{w}\rangle \\ &= \langle (\Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i^* \mid\vec{e_i}\rangle) \mid \\ &= \langle \Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i^* \vec{e_i}\mid \\ &= \Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i \langle\vec{e_i}\ \mid \tag{4} \end{align} \]

Now, if do \(\langle\vec{w}\mid\vec{v}\rangle\), we can use expressions (2) and (4) to arrive at the following: \[ \begin{align} \langle\vec{w}\mid\vec{v}\rangle &= (\Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i \langle\vec{e_i}\ \mid) \mid\vec{v}\rangle \\ &= \Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i \langle\vec{e_i}\ \mid\vec{v}\rangle \tag{5} \end{align} \] Knowing the resolution of identity, which says that the sum of all projectors of the orthonormal basis vectors, \(\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle\langle\vec{e_i}\mid\) results in the identity matrix \(\mathbf{I}\), we can manipulate equation (1) like so: \[ \begin{align} \vec{v} &= \Sigma_{i=1}^n c_i\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle \\ &= \mathbf{I}\mid\vec{v}\rangle = (\Sigma_{i=1}^n \mid\vec{e_i}\rangle\langle\vec{e_i}\mid)\mid\vec{v}\rangle \\ &= \Sigma_{i=1}^n \mid\vec{e_i}\rangle\langle\vec{e_i}\mid\vec{v}\rangle \\ &= \Sigma_{i=1}^n \langle\vec{e_i}\mid\vec{v}\rangle \mid\vec{e_i}\rangle \\ c_i &= \langle\vec{e_i}\mid\vec{v}\rangle \tag{6} \end{align} \] So, substituting (4) into (5), we can now write: \[ \begin{align} \langle\vec{w}\mid\vec{v}\rangle &= \Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i \langle\vec{e_i}\ \mid\vec{v}\rangle \\ &= \Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i c_i \end{align} \] This is exactly the expression defined in (3)! So, \(\langle\vec{w}\mid\vec{v}\rangle = f\mid\vec{v}\rangle\).

Since we can define any vector in \(V\) as we did in (1), we can always form a vector \(\mid\vec{w}\rangle\) and arrive at the result in (6). Essentially, we have shown that every single linear function \(f\) can be represented by a unique \(\langle\vec{w}\mid \in V^*\) which is the dual of the ket \(\mid\vec{w}\rangle \in V\).

Properties of the Dual Correspondence

Now we know that every linear functional has a corresponding bra in the dual space of \(V\), or formally: \[ \begin{align} \forall f \in V^*, f &= C\mid\vec{w}\rangle\\ &= C [\Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i^* \mid\vec{e_i}\rangle]\\ &=C[\Sigma_{i=1}^n (f\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle)^*\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle] \end{align} \] Which tells us that we can also define a \(C^{-1}\) to recover \(\mid\vec{w}\rangle\) from \(f\): \[C^{-1}: V^*\to V, f \mapsto \Sigma^n_{i=1}\alpha_i^* \mid\vec{e_i}\rangle = \Sigma_{i=1}^n (f\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle)^*\mid\vec{e_i}\rangle\] indicating that \(dim(V) = dim(V^*)\).

Since \(C\) maps all kets to a bra in this way, we can see that \(C\) is also anti-linear: \[C(\alpha\mid\vec{v}\rangle + \beta \mid\vec{w}\rangle) = \alpha^*\langle\vec{v}\mid + \beta^*\langle\vec{w}\mid\]
