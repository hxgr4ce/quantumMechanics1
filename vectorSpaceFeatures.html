Features of a Vector Space

Linear Dependence

A set of vectors \(\{\vec{v_i}\}_{i=1,...,n}, \vec{v_i} \in V\), where \(V\) is a vector space over field \(F\), is linearly dependent if some non-trivial linear combination of each vector in the set equals \(\vec{0}\). Formally, it is linearly dependent only if: \[\exists \{c_i\}_{i=1,...,n}, c_i \in F, \exists c_i \neq 0 \mid \sum_{i=1}^n c_i\vec{v_i}=\vec{0}\]

Example:

  • The vectors \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), \( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), and \( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \) are linearly independent.
  • The vectors \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), \( \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), and \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \) are linearly dependent.

Generators

A set of vectors \(\{\vec{g_i}\}\) in vector space \(V\) over field \(F\) generates \(V\) if all linear combinations of \(\{\vec{g_i}\}\) describe an element in \(V\). Formally, \(\{\vec{g_i}\}\) is a generator of \(V\) if: \[\forall \{c_i\}, c_i \in F, \sum_{i}c_i\vec{g_i}=\vec{v}, \vec{v} \in V \]

Example: \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), \( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), and \( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \) generate the vector space \((\mathbb{R}^3, \mathbb{R})\).

Note: The generator of a space is also called a spanning set, and the space generated by set \(S\) is also called span(\(S\))

Subspaces

A subset of vectors \(W\) from a vector space \(V\) over field \(F\) is also a vector space if it has the following properties:

  • Existence of \(e_+\): \(\vec{0} \in W\)
  • Closure under addition: \(\forall \vec{u}, \vec{v} \in W, \vec{u}+\vec{v} \in W\)
  • Closure under multiplication by scalar: \(\forall c \in F, \forall \vec{v} \in W, c\vec{v} \in W\)
\(W\) is then called a subspace of \(V\).

Examples:

  • The set of all vectors in vector space \(V\) along a line which passes through the origin is a one-dimensional subspace of \(V\). This can be generated by one vector.
  • The xy-plane is a two-dimensional subspace of \((\mathbb{R}^3, \mathbb{R})\). One set of generators for this subspace is the vectors \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \) and \( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), but you can also generate the exact same subspace with the set of vectors \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), \( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \), and \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \).

Note 1: A set of generators for a subspace of \(V\) does not have to be linearly independent, as seen in one example above.

Note 2: You cannot generate a subspace with a higher dimension than \(V\).

Basis of a Vector Space

If the set of vectors generating a vector space \(V\) is linearly independent, then that set is the basis of \(V\).

The cardinality (number of elements) in the basis of \(V\) equals \(V\)'s dimension, and all vectors in the basis are orthogonal to each other.

Example: \( \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \) and \( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \) are the basis of \((\mathbb{R}^2, \mathbb{R})\).

Sums of Subspaces

If \(U\) and \(W\) are subspaces of \((V, F)\) (vector space \(V\) over field \(F\)), then \(U+W=\{\vec{u}+\vec{w}\mid\vec{u}\in U, \vec{w} \in W\}\) is also a subspace of \(V\).

If \(\forall \vec{v} \in U+W, \exists! \vec{u} \in U, \vec{w} \in W \mid \vec{u}+\vec{w}=\vec{v}\), then \(U+W\) is more specifically called a direct sum, \(U\oplus V\).

Say that \(U\) has basis \(\{u_i\}_{i=1,...,n}\) and \(W\) has basis \(\{w_i\}_{i=1,...,m}\). Then \(U \oplus V \iff \{u_1,...,u_n, w_1,...,w_m\} \) is linearly independent.

Examples:

  • If lines \(L_1\) and \(L_2\) intersect at the origin and are not parallel (so are linearly independent), \(L_1\oplus L_2\).
  • If two planes in \((\mathbb{R}^3, \mathbb{R})\), \(P_1\) and \(P_2\), intersect on a line \(\neq \vec{0}\), the subspace sum \(P_1+P_2\) is a subspace of \((\mathbb{R}^3, \mathbb{R})\) but is not a direct sum, since they are not linearly independent.

    (It is clearly impossible for two planes in \((\mathbb{R}^3, \mathbb{R})\) to intersect and not be linearly independent:
    A plane is two-dimensional and its basis is made of two vectors. So two planes must exist in four dimensions, and have four basis vectors between them. But \((\mathbb{R}^3, \mathbb{R})\) is a three-dimensional space, meaning one basis vector from each plane must overlap, resulting in a line of intersection.)

Note: More succinctly, \(U \oplus W \iff U \cap W = \{\vec{0}\}\), or, \(U+W\) is a direct sum if and only if they have a trivial intersection.

This comes from the fact that if \(U+W\) was not a direct sum, then: \[\exists \vec{v} \in U+W \mid (\vec{u_i}, \vec{w_i}) \neq (\vec{u_j}, \vec{w_j}), \vec{v} = \vec{u_i} + \vec{w_i} = \vec{u_j}, \vec{w_j} \implies \vec{u_i} - \vec{u_j}=\vec{w_j} - \vec{w_i}\] And since \(U\) and \(W\) are both subspaces of \(V\) and thus closed under addition, \(\vec{u_i} - \vec{u_j} \in U\) and \(\vec{w_j} - \vec{w_i} \in W\), meaning \(U\) and \(W\) have non-trivial intersection \(\vec{u_i} - \vec{u_j}=\vec{w_j} - \vec{w_i} \in U+W\).
