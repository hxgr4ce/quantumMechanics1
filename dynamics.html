The Evolution of Quantum States Over Time

Since it is the generator of unitary time translation, we know that the Hamiltonian operator \(\hat{H}\) is crucial to understanding how quantum state evolve over time. We now look into the spectral properties of the Hamiltonian operator and discuss how an isolated quantum state evolves over time.

Stationary States

Systems in a stationary state stay in a stationary state. For eigenstates \(|\phi_i\rangle\) of \(\hat{H}\): \[\hat{H}|\phi_i\rangle = E_i|\phi_i\rangle\] So, if the initial state of a system is \(|\psi(t=0)\rangle = |\phi_i\rangle\): \[\begin{align} |\psi(t)\rangle &= e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}|\phi_i\rangle \\ &= e^{-iE_it/\hbar}|\phi_i\rangle \\ \end{align}\] Recalling our quantum postulates that tell us that a state is a ray-- that is a vector whose length does not matter-- this above result tells us that if we prepare a system to be in an eigenstate of \(\hat{H}\), \(|\phi_i\rangle\) it will continue to be in that state as long as it remains an isolated system. This is because our \(e^{-iE_it/\hbar}\) term simply changes the length of the vector along \(|\phi_i\rangle\).

We can see this another way: \[\begin{align} \langle\hat{A}\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \langle\psi(t)|\hat{A}|\psi(t)\rangle \\ &= e^{iE_it/\hbar}\langle\phi_i|\hat{A}|\phi_i\rangle e^{-iE_it/\hbar} \\ &= \langle\phi_i|\hat{A}|\phi_i\rangle \\ &= \langle\hat{A}\rangle_{\psi(0)} \end{align}\] The expectation of a system in a stationary state... is stationary. The expectation value of the isolated system does not change with time! The final state after measurement also does not change: \[\begin{align} |\psi\rangle &\mapsto \hat{P}|\psi\rangle \\ |\psi(t)\rangle &\mapsto \hat{P}|\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{P}e^{-iE_it/\hbar}|\phi_i\rangle \\ &= e^{-iE_it/\hbar}\hat{P}|\phi_i\rangle \\ \end{align}\] The end result is a ray that is along the same direction that we would have from measuring the initial state \(|\phi_i\rangle\) right away, \(\hat{P}|\phi_i\rangle\). For this reason, the eigenstate of \(\hat{H}\) are called stationary states.

Non-stationary Initial States

Systems not in a stationary state change over time. What if our initial state is not a stationary state? We find that the state of the system does change over time: \[\begin{align} |\psi(t)\rangle &= \sum_i |\phi_i\rangle\langle\phi_i|\psi(t)\rangle = \sum_i|\phi_i\rangle c_i(t) \\ |\psi(t)\rangle &= e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}\sum_i|\phi_i\rangle c_i(0) \\ &= \sum_ie^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}|\phi_i\rangle c_i(0) \\ &= \sum_i|\phi_i\rangle c_i(t) \\ c_i(t) &= e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}c_i(0) \end{align}\] We see that the expansion coefficient \(c_i(t)\) for each corresponding eigenbasis vector of \(\hat{H}\), \(|\phi_i\rangle\), changes over time at the rate \(\frac{E_i}{\hbar}\). Because the eigenvalue \(E_i\) is different for each \(|\phi_i\rangle\), eacn component of \(|\psi(t)\rangle\) changes differently over time, hence \(|\psi(t)\rangle\) is not the same state as \(|\psi(0)\rangle\).

Systems not in a stationary state have oscillating expectation values (mostly). The expectation value is also not constant: \[\begin{align} \langle\hat{A}\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \langle\psi(t)|\hat{A}|\psi(t)\rangle \\ &= \sum_{ij} e^{iE_it/\hbar}c_i^*(0)\langle\phi_i|\hat{A}|\phi_j\rangle e^{-iE_jt/\hbar}c_j(0) \end{align}\] If our basis is the eigenstates of \(\hat{H}\), an element in \(\hat{A}\)'s matrix, \(A_{ij}=\langle\phi_i|\hat{A}|\phi_j\rangle\). Also, call \(\omega_{ji} = \frac{E_j-E_i}{\hbar}\) the Bohr angular frequency. We can then rewrite: \[\langle\hat{A}\rangle_{\psi(t)} = \sum_{ij}A_{ij}c_i^*(0)c_j(0)e^{-i\omega_{ji}t}\] and then break this down further: \[\begin{align} \langle\hat{A}\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \sum_{j=i}A_{ii}|c_i(0)|^2 + \sum_{j\gt i}A_{ij}c_i^*(0)c_j(0)e^{-i\omega_{ji}t} + \sum_{j\lt i} A_{ij}c_i^*(0)c_j(0)e^{-i\omega_{ji}t} \tag{1} \end{align}\] We can flip the indices of the last term (swapping \(i\) and \(j\)) to get: \[ \sum_{j\lt i} A_{ij}c_i^*(0)c_j(0)e^{-i\omega_{ji}t} \to \sum_{i\lt j} A_{ji}c_j^*(0)c_i(0)e^{-i\omega_{ij}t} \] We know that \(A_{ji}=A_{ij}^*\) and that \(\omega_{ji} = \frac{E_j-E_i}{\hbar}\), so \(\omega_{ij} = -\omega_{ji}\), so we can actually write this entire thing as: \[\begin{align} \sum_{i\lt j} A_{ji}c_j^*(0)c_i(0)e^{-i\omega_{ij}t} &= \sum_{i\lt j} A_{ij}^*c_j^*(0)c_i(0)e^{i\omega_{ji}t} \\ &= (\sum_{i\lt j} A_{ij}c_j(0)c_i^*(0)e^{-i\omega_{ji}t})^* \end{align}\] which is the complex conjugate of the second term in (1). By definition, a complex value \(z\) plus its cojugate is \(2Re\{z\}\) so we can write: \[\begin{align} \langle\hat{A}\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \sum_{j=i}A_{ii}|c_i(0)|^2 + 2 Re\{\sum_{j\gt i}A_{ij}c_i^*(0)c_j(0)e^{-i\omega_{ji}t}\} \end{align}\] We can also write any complex value \(z\) as \(|z|\cdot e^{i\theta}\), so we can write \(A_{ij} c_i^*(0) c_j(0)\) as \(|A_{ij} c_i^*(0) c_j(0)| \cdot e^{i\theta_{ij}}\): \[\begin{align} \langle\hat{A}\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \sum_{j=i}A_{ii}|c_i(0)|^2 + 2 \sum_{j\gt i}|A_{ij} c_i^*(0) c_j(0)| \cdot Re\{ e^{i\theta_{ij}}e^{-i\omega_{ji}t}\} \\ &= \sum_{j=i}A_{ii}|c_i(0)|^2 + 2 \sum_{j\gt i}|A_{ij} c_i^*(0) c_j(0)| \cdot \cos(\omega_{ji}t-\theta_{ij}) \\ \end{align}\] This first term, \(\sum_{j=i}A_{ii}|c_i(0)|^2\), is static since it does not vary with time, and the second term is a sinusoid with period \(T=\frac{2\pi}{\omega_{ji}}\) and is offset a distance of \(\frac{\phi_{ij}}{\omega_{ji}}\) from the origin. So, the expectation value is indeed not constant; it oscillates over time!

A special case! The only special case where the expectation value does not vary (aside from when the initial state is a stationary state, like we saw earlier) is when \(\hat A\) commutes with \(\hat H\):

In order for the expectation value to be zero, the term that varies with time should be zero. However, it's impossible to expect that \(\omega_{ji} = \frac{E_j-E_i}{\hbar}\) is always zero when we explicitly state that we're ranging over indices \(j\gt i\). It's also impossible to expect that \(\theta_{ij}\) changes exactly so that \(\omega_{ji}-\theta_{ij}\) does not vary, or that either \(c_i(0)\) or \(c_j(0)\) are always 0. Hence we have to rely on \(A_{ij}\).

When is \(A_{ij}\) equal to zero? Recall that we stated that our basis was the eigenstates of \(\hat H\) and that the elements in \(\hat A\)'s matrix could be written as \(A_{ij} = \langle \phi_i|\hat A|\phi_j\rangle\) for eigenstates \(|\phi_i\rangle\).

Therefore, it is readily apparent that we can only guarantee that \(A_{ij} = \langle \phi_i|\hat A|\phi_j\rangle = 0\) for \(j\neq i\) only when \([\hat A, \hat H]=0\). That is, the matrix \(A\) is diagonalizable in \(\hat H\)'s eigenbasis, meaning that they share a common basis of eigenvectors, so they commute. Mathematically: \[\begin{align} \langle \phi_i|[\hat A, \hat H]|\phi_j\rangle &= \langle\phi_i|0|\phi_j\rangle = 0 \\ &= \langle \phi_i|\hat A\hat H-\hat H\hat A|\phi_j\rangle \\ &= \langle \phi_i|\hat A\hat H|\phi_j\rangle - \langle\phi_i|\hat H\hat A|\phi_j\rangle \\ &= \langle \phi_i|\hat A (E_j|\phi_j\rangle) - \langle\hat H^\dagger\phi_i|\hat A|\phi_j\rangle \\ &= \langle E_j\phi_i|\hat A |\phi_j\rangle - \langle\hat H\phi_i|\hat A|\phi_j\rangle = \langle E_j\phi_i|\hat A |\phi_j\rangle - \langle E_i\phi_i|\hat A|\phi_j\rangle \\ 0 &= (E_j-E_i)\langle\phi_i|\hat A |\phi_j\rangle \end{align}\] As we said before, \(j\neq i\), so \(E_j\neq E_i\), meaning that \(\langle\phi_i|\hat A |\phi_j\rangle = 0\) if \(j\neq i\), so the matrix \(A\) is diagonal in \(\hat H\)'s eigenbasis and \([\hat A,\hat H]=0\).

Alternatively, we can think about it like this: \[\begin{align} \langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \langle\psi(t)|\hat A|\psi(t)\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi(0)|e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi(0)|\hat A e^{i\hat H t/\hbar}e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle \tag{2} \\ &= \langle\psi(0)|\hat A|\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(0)} \end{align}\] We are only able to move to (2) if \(\hat H\) and \(\hat A\) commute, which allows us to rearrange our operators. So, we can only say that \(\langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(t)} = \langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(0)}\) for any arbitrary \(t\) if \([\hat H,\hat A]=0\). \[[\hat A,\hat H]=0\implies\langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(t)} \text{ is constant over t.}\]

Expectation Values Over Time (Generally)

We can see the relationship between how \(\langle\hat A\rangle\) changes over time and \([\hat H,\hat A]\) by doing the following: \[\begin{align} \frac{d}{dt}\langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \frac{d}{dt}\langle\psi(0)|e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi(0)|\frac{d}{dt}(e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar})|\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi(0)|\frac{de^{i\hat H t/\hbar}}{dt} \hat A e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} + e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A \frac{de^{-i\hat H t/\hbar}}{dt} |\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \langle\psi(0)|\frac{i\hat H}{\hbar}e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} + e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A \frac{-i\hat H}{\hbar}e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle\psi(0)|\hat H e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} + e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A (-\hat H)e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle\psi(0)|e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat H\hat A e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} - e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} \hat A \hat He^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle\psi(0)|e^{i\hat H t/\hbar} [\hat H, \hat A] e^{-i\hat H t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle \\ &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle\psi(t)|[\hat H, \hat A]|\psi(t)\rangle \\ \frac{d}{dt}\langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle[\hat H, \hat A]\rangle_{\psi(t)} \tag{3}\\ \end{align}\] Clearly, if \(\hat A\) and \(\hat H\) commute, then the expectation value does not change over time; its derivative with respect to time is zero.

We can apply this as follows: Say that we have a free particle in one dimension with \(\hat H=\frac{\hat p^2}{2m} + V(\hat x)\). If \(\hat A=\hat p\), then \[\begin{align} \langle x|[\hat H,\hat p] &= \langle x|[\frac{\hat p^2}{2m}+V(\hat x), \hat p] \\ &= \langle x|[\frac{\hat p^2}{2m}, \hat p] + \langle x|[V(\hat x), \hat p] = 0 + \langle x|[V(\hat x), \hat p] \\ &= \langle x|V(\hat x)\hat p - \langle x|\hat p V(\hat x) \\ &= V(x)\langle x|\hat p - (-i\hbar)\frac{d}{dx} (\langle x|V(\hat x)) \\ &= V(x)(-i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}\langle x|) + i\hbar\frac{d}{dx} (V(x)\langle x|) \\ &= -i\hbar V(x)\frac{d\langle x|}{dx} + i\hbar (\frac{dV(x)}{dx}\langle x| + \frac{d\langle x|}{dx} V(\hat x)) \\ &= i\hbar \frac{dV(x)}{dx}\langle x| \\ [\hat H,\hat p] &= i\hbar \frac{dV(x)}{dx} \end{align}\] We can plug this final result into (3): \[\begin{align} \frac{d}{dt}\langle\hat A\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle[\hat H, \hat A]\rangle_{\psi(t)} \\ \frac{d}{dt}\langle\hat p\rangle_{\psi(t)} &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle[\hat H, \hat p]\rangle_{\psi(t)} \\ &= \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle i\hbar \frac{dV(x)}{dx}\rangle_{\psi(t)} \\ &= -\langle \frac{dV(x)}{dx}\rangle_{\psi(t)} \\ \end{align}\] which, may seem familiar if you know Ehrenfest's Theorem. Note that this mirrors \(\frac{dp}{dt}=ma=F=-\frac{dV}{dx}\), from classical mechanics.

Momentum and the Hamiltonian

Now that we know a little more about how states evolve over time than we did before, we now turn our attention to the Hamiltonian as promised. We use the free particle in one dimension with mass \(m\). Classically, \(H=\frac{p^2}{2m}\), so we can say that \(\hat H=\frac{\hat p^2}{2m}\), which tells us that \([\hat H, \hat p]=0\) and that the eigenstates of \(\hat p\) are eigenstates of \(\hat H\).

Our generalized eigenstates of \(\hat p\) are such that \(\hat p|p\rangle = p|p\rangle\), so \(\sigma(\hat p) = \mathbb{R}\) and \(\langle p|p'\rangle = \delta(p-p')\), and \(\hat H|p\rangle = \frac{p^2}{2m}|p\rangle\), so \(\sigma(\hat H) = \mathbb{R_0^+}\) (because the \(p^2\) guarantees that eigenvalues are non-negative).

Since \(\hat H\) is in terms of \(\hat p\), we take a short detour to discuss the momentum operator. What does \(\hat p|p\rangle\) look like in the position basis? \[\begin{align} \langle x | \hat p|p\rangle &= p\langle x|p\rangle \\ &= -i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}\phi_p(x) = p \phi_p(x) \\ (-i\hbar\frac{d}{dx} - p) \phi_p(x) &= 0 \end{align}\] if we use \(\phi_p(x) = \langle x|p\rangle\). This is a linear first order ordinary differential equation that has a known solution: \[\begin{align} \frac{d\phi_p(x)}{dx}\frac{1}{\phi_p(x)} &= \frac{ip}{\hbar} \\ \frac{d \ln{\phi_p(x)}}{dx} &= \frac{ip}{\hbar} \\ \ln\phi_p(x) &= \frac{ipx}{\hbar} + c_p \\ \phi_p(x) &= e^{c_p}e^{ipx/\hbar} \\ &= N_p e^{ipx/\hbar} \end{align}\] We call \(e^{c_p}\) some normalization constation \(N_p\), and determine its value by enforcing \(\langle p|p'\rangle = \delta(p-p')\). We insert the identity \(\hat 1 = \int dx |x\rangle\langle x|\): \[\begin{align} \langle p|p'\rangle &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx\langle p|x\rangle\langle x|p'\rangle \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx\phi_p^*(x)\phi_{p'}(x) \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx (N_p e^{ipx/\hbar})^*N_{p'} e^{ip'x/\hbar} \\ &= N_p^*N_{p'} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx (e^{ipx/\hbar})^* e^{ip'x/\hbar} = N_p^*N_{p'} \int dx e^{-ipx/\hbar} e^{ip'x/\hbar} \\ &= N_p^*N_{p'} \int_{-\infty}^\infty dx e^{i(p'-p)x/\hbar} \\ &= \hbar N_p^*N_{p'} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx}{\hbar} e^{i(p'-p)x/\hbar} \\ \text{if } u &= \frac{x}{\hbar}: \\ &= \hbar N_p^*N_{p'} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{i(p'-p)u} du \\ &= \hbar N^*_pN_{p'}\cdot 2\pi\delta(p'-p) \end{align}\] Using the known solution \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{ikx}dx = 2\pi\delta(k)\).
